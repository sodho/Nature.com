Our Mission

We conserve the lands and waters on which all life depends

Founded in 1951, The Nature Conservancy is a global conservation organization dedicated to conserving the lands and waters on which all life depends. Guided by science, we create innovative, on-the-ground solutions to our world's toughest challenges so that nature and people can thrive together.

We're tackling climate change, conserving lands, waters and oceans at unprecedented scale, providing food and water sustainably and helping make cities more sustainable. Working in 72 countries, we use a collaborative approach that engages local communities, governments, the private sector, and other partners.

Our Story

The Nature Conservancy was founded in 1951 by a group of scientists and environmentalists who wanted to take direct action to protect and preserve natural areas around the world. What began as a small organization with a big idea has grown into one of the most effective and wide-reaching environmental organizations in the world.

Our founders pioneered the concept of using private funds to purchase ecologically important lands for conservationâ€”an approach that has since been replicated around the globe. Today, we work in all 50 U.S. states and more than 70 countries, using science to guide our conservation efforts and collaborating with local communities, governments, businesses, and other partners.

General Questions

What is The Nature Conservancy?

The Nature Conservancy is a global environmental nonprofit working to conserve the lands and waters on which all life depends. Founded in 1951, we operate in all 50 U.S. states and more than 70 countries.

How is The Nature Conservancy different from other environmental organizations?

We take a science-based, non-confrontational approach to conservation. We work collaboratively with communities, governments, businesses, and other partners to develop practical solutions to environmental challenges.

Innovative Approaches to Forest Restoration

June 15, 2023 Dr. Sarah Greenfield 5 min read

Forests are the lungs of our planet, but they face unprecedented threats from deforestation, climate change, and invasive species. Traditional reforestation methods often struggle to keep pace with these challenges. That's why conservationists are pioneering innovative approaches to restore forests faster and more effectively than ever before.

The Miyawaki Method: Creating Mini-Forests

Developed by Japanese botanist Akira Miyawaki, this technique creates dense, biodiverse forests in small urban spaces. By planting native species close together in specially prepared soil, these "mini-forests" grow 10 times faster and become self-sustaining within just 20 years.

"The Miyawaki method mimics natural forest regeneration processes but accelerates them dramatically. We've seen incredible results in urban areas where space is limited."

Drone Reforestation: Planting from the Sky

In hard-to-reach areas, drone technology is revolutionizing reforestation. Specially designed drones can plant up to 100,000 seed pods per day, with each pod containing nutrients and moisture-retaining materials to boost survival rates.

Fungal Networks: Nature's Internet

Scientists are harnessing mycorrhizal fungi networks that connect tree roots underground. These "wood wide web" systems allow trees to share nutrients and information. By introducing these fungi to new plantings, we're creating resilient forest ecosystems that support themselves.

Key Benefits of Modern Forest Restoration

  • 300% faster growth compared to traditional methods
  • Up to 90% survival rate for new plantings
  • 30% greater carbon sequestration capacity
  • Enhanced biodiversity with 5x more species

As we implement these innovative techniques across our restoration projects, we're not just planting treesâ€”we're building resilient ecosystems that will thrive for generations. The future of forests looks brighter thanks to these groundbreaking approaches.

The Future of Coral Reefs in a Warming World

May 28, 2023 Dr. Marcus Ocean 6 min read

Coral reefs, often called the rainforests of the sea, are facing unprecedented challenges from climate change. Rising ocean temperatures cause coral bleaching, where corals expel the symbiotic algae that provide them with color and nutrients. But new research offers hope for these vital ecosystems.

Super Corals: Nature's Resilient Survivors

Our research team has identified several coral species that demonstrate remarkable resilience to warmer waters. These "super corals" possess genetic adaptations that allow them to withstand temperature fluctuations that would bleach most other corals.

"In the Red Sea, we've discovered corals thriving in waters 2-3Â°C warmer than their typical habitats. Understanding their resilience could be key to saving reefs worldwide."

Assisted Evolution: Speeding Up Nature's Process

Scientists are experimenting with selective breeding techniques to enhance coral resilience. By cross-breading heat-tolerant corals and exposing larvae to gradually increasing temperatures, we're accelerating the natural adaptation process.

3D Printing Artificial Reef Structures

Innovative technology is helping restore damaged reefs through 3D-printed structures that mimic natural coral formations. These structures provide immediate habitat for marine life while serving as foundations for new coral growth.

Conservation Strategies Making a Difference

  • Establishing marine protected areas to reduce human impact
  • Reducing land-based pollution that harms reef systems
  • Developing coral nurseries to grow resilient species
  • Implementing sustainable fishing practices

While challenges remain, these innovative approaches offer real hope for coral reefs. By combining scientific research with conservation action, we're working to ensure these vibrant ecosystems continue to sustain both people and wildlife for generations to come.

Water Conservation Strategies for Cities

May 10, 2023 Dr. Lisa Waters 4 min read

As urban populations grow and climate patterns shift, cities worldwide face increasing water scarcity challenges. Innovative water conservation strategies are essential for sustainable urban development. Here are some groundbreaking approaches cities are implementing to secure their water future.

Green Infrastructure: Nature's Water Filtration

Urban areas are replacing traditional concrete infrastructure with green solutions that mimic natural water cycles. Green roofs, permeable pavements, and bioswales capture rainwater where it falls, reducing runoff and replenishing groundwater supplies.

"In Philadelphia, green infrastructure projects have reduced stormwater runoff by 1.7 billion gallons annually while creating vibrant community spaces."

Smart Water Networks: Technology to the Rescue

Advanced sensor networks and AI-powered systems are helping cities detect leaks, monitor water quality, and optimize distribution in real-time. These smart water networks can reduce water loss by up to 25% and significantly cut operational costs.

Water Recycling: Closing the Loop

Forward-thinking cities are implementing advanced water recycling systems that treat wastewater to drinking water standards. Singapore's NEWater program now meets 40% of the nation's water needs through recycling, with plans to reach 55% by 2060.

Key Urban Water Conservation Strategies

  • Implement tiered water pricing to encourage conservation
  • Replace water-intensive landscaping with native drought-resistant plants
  • Install smart irrigation systems that respond to weather conditions
  • Promote water-efficient appliances through rebate programs
  • Develop urban wetlands that naturally filter and store water

As cities continue to innovate, these water conservation strategies not only address scarcity but also create more resilient, livable urban environments. By working with nature rather than against it, we're building sustainable water systems for the future.

Climate Change Program

Global Initiative 150+ Projects

Climate change is the defining environmental challenge of our time. Our Climate Change Program takes a comprehensive approach to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and help nature adapt through natural climate solutions. Our approach combines science, policy, and on-the-ground conservation to:

Natural Climate Solutions

Nature itself offers powerful solutions to climate change. Forests, wetlands, and grasslands sequester carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Our program focuses on protecting and restoring these natural carbon sinks:

"Protecting and restoring forests, wetlands, and grasslands could provide more than one-third of the emissions reductions needed by 2030 to keep global warming below 2Â°C."

Key Focus Areas

Forest Conservation

We're working to prevent deforestation across critical landscapes like the Amazon, Congo Basin, and Southeast Asia. By partnering with local communities and governments, we're creating economic alternatives to deforestation.

Coastal Ecosystem Restoration

Mangroves, salt marshes, and seagrass meadows store carbon at rates up to 10 times higher than terrestrial forests. Our coastal restoration projects protect these vital ecosystems while creating natural barriers against storm surges.

Sustainable Agriculture

We're helping farmers implement practices that reduce emissions, improve soil health, and increase resilience. Regenerative agriculture techniques can transform farmland from a carbon source to a carbon sink.

Program Impact

  • Protected over 50 million acres of high-carbon forests
  • Restored 250,000 acres of coastal wetlands
  • Trained 15,000 farmers in climate-smart agriculture
  • Prevented 500 million tons of CO2 emissions

Through science, collaboration, and innovation, our Climate Change Program is creating a more resilient future for both people and nature. Join us in this critical work to address the climate crisis.

Land Conservation Program

Terrestrial Ecosystems 30 Countries

Healthy lands are essential for clean air, fresh water, food security, and biodiversity. Our Land Conservation Program protects vital habitats through science-based conservation strategies and innovative partnerships.

Conservation Strategies

We employ a range of approaches to protect land, tailored to local contexts and conservation priorities:

Protected Areas

Establishing and effectively managing national parks, wildlife refuges, and other protected areas that conserve critical habitats and species.

Conservation Easements

Working with private landowners to place legally binding restrictions on their property to prevent development while keeping land in private ownership.

"Conservation easements have protected over 2 million acres of vital habitat in the United States alone, creating wildlife corridors and preserving agricultural lands."

Indigenous Land Management

Supporting indigenous communities in securing land rights and implementing traditional conservation practices that have proven effective for centuries.

Focus Ecosystems

  • Grasslands: Protecting North America's prairies and Africa's savannas
  • Forests: Conserving tropical rainforests and temperate woodlands
  • Deserts: Preserving arid ecosystems and their unique biodiversity
  • Wetlands: Safeguarding marshes, bogs, and floodplains

Program Impact

Since 1951, our land conservation efforts have:

  • Protected over 125 million acres worldwide
  • Established 1,500+ protected areas
  • Supported 200+ indigenous land management initiatives
  • Secured conservation easements on 5 million acres

Through science, collaboration, and innovation, we're ensuring that vital lands remain intact for future generations. Healthy lands mean healthy people and a healthy planet.

Water Protection Program

Freshwater Conservation 40 Major Watersheds

Freshwater ecosystems cover less than 1% of Earth's surface but support 10% of all known species and provide essential resources for human communities. Our Water Protection Program works to safeguard these vital resources through science-based conservation strategies.

Integrated Watershed Management

We take a holistic approach to water conservation, recognizing that rivers, lakes, wetlands, and groundwater are interconnected systems. Our strategies include:

Protecting Headwaters

Conserving forests and wetlands in upstream areas that naturally filter water and regulate flow. Healthy headwaters are critical for clean water downstream.

Restoring Rivers

Removing obsolete dams, reconnecting floodplains, and restoring natural river flows to revive aquatic ecosystems and improve water quality.

"In the Colorado River Basin, our water-sharing agreements have restored flows while meeting human needsâ€”a model for water-stressed regions worldwide."

Sustainable Water Use

We work with farmers, industries, and cities to implement water-saving technologies and practices:

  • Precision irrigation systems that reduce agricultural water use by 30-50%
  • Industrial water recycling programs
  • Urban conservation initiatives including green infrastructure
  • Water markets that create economic incentives for conservation

Program Impact

Our water protection efforts have:

  • Protected 10,000 miles of rivers
  • Restored 500,000 acres of wetlands
  • Secured water rights for environmental flows in 25 major rivers
  • Reduced agricultural water consumption by 1.2 trillion gallons annually
  • Improved water quality for 50 million people

By working with nature and innovative partners, we're ensuring that freshwater ecosystems continue to sustain both people and wildlife for generations to come.
